# Plan: The Defense Tax in Allied Perspective

## Research question
Does the evidence support cutting, holding, or raising US defense funding — and how
should a "defense lane" express the level when the right number is a strategic
judgment, not a fiscal calculation?

## Claim
US national defense costs **$916,140M in FY2025 (13.1% of federal outlays)** and runs
**~3.0% of GDP on the OMB budget-function (050) basis**, or **~3.2–3.4% on the
SIPRI/NATO definition** (which is broader). That exceeds the **European NATO norm
(~2.0%)** and the old 2% floor, but sits far below US Cold-War levels (~14% of GDP at
the 1953 Korean-War peak, ~6.5–7% in the Reagan buildup). The live policy driver is
the **25 Jun 2025 Hague commitment: 5% of GDP by 2035 = 3.5% core defence + 1.5%
broader security**. The honest conclusion is that defense has **no fiscally "fair
rate"**: the level is set by strategic/threat judgment. The factors **bound** a
defensible band — **2.0–3.5% of GDP** — but do not **determine** a point inside it.
Direction: hold-to-modest-increase within a policy band, not a cut justified on
peer-norm grounds alone.

## Honesty hooks (baked in to clear round 1)
- Label **every** ratio: %-of-outlays vs %-of-GDP; OMB-function (050) basis vs
  SIPRI/NATO definition (do not merge the series).
- Present the recommendation as a **band (2.0–3.5%)**, not a point; say plainly the
  factors bound but do not determine the level (normative/strategic, not a finding).
- Distribution/incidence: defense is **general-revenue + borrowing financed**, not an
  earmarked contributory lane — so "who pays" is the whole income-tax/borrowing base;
  note the borrowed share.
- Forward trajectory: the Hague path is a **commitment**, not appropriated money;
  3.5% core would be a real-terms increase over today's ~3.0%.
- Peer caveat: "exceeds the European norm" is a burden-sharing fact, not by itself a
  case to cut — the US role (global posture, nuclear umbrella) differs from a single
  European ally's.
