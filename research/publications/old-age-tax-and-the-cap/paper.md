# The Old-Age Tax and the Cap: Benchmarking the US Social-Security Levy Against Peer Pension Systems

**TAXLANE Research — panel-reviewed (draft, round 0).** An educational reform model.
Figures are sourced to `docs/sources/source-version-ledger.md`; Trustees and CBO
figures are projections under stated assumptions. Rate recommendations are reform
proposals; no tax advice is given.

## Abstract

The US funds old-age and survivors insurance through a dedicated payroll tax, and the
debate over its solvency usually pits "raise the rate" against "cut benefits." Both
miss the distinctive feature of the American system: the **wage cap**. US public
pension spending is roughly **peer-normal — 7.3% of GDP versus the OECD's 8.1%**
[`SRC-OECD-PENSIONS-2025`] — and the combined **OASDI rate (12.4%) is below peers**
(Germany 18.6%, Japan 18.3%) [`SRC-OECD-PENSIONS-2025`]. But the US uniquely **caps the
taxed wage base** ($176,100 in 2025; $184,500 in 2026), so **~17–18% of covered
earnings escape the tax entirely** [`SRC-SSA-TRUSTEES-2026`, `SRC-SSA-OACT`]. The OASI
trust fund is projected to deplete in **Q4 2032** (combined OASDI ~2034), after which
roughly **83% of scheduled benefits** would be payable from incoming taxes, declining
toward 65% by 2100; the 75-year actuarial deficit is **4.42% of taxable payroll
(~1.5% of GDP)** [`SRC-SSA-TRUSTEES-2026`]. We argue the fair lever is the **cap, not
the rate**: lifting or eliminating the wage cap is **progressive** and is scored to
close a large share of the gap (CBO: covering 90% of earnings ~$0.8T/10yr ≈ 20% of the
shortfall; taxing earnings over $250,000 ~$1.6T/10yr ≈ 70%) [`SRC-CBO-BUDGET-OPTIONS`],
whereas a rate increase is regressive and a benefit cut falls on retirees. Modernizing
the old-age tax means broadening its **base** to match peer practice while protecting
benefits — not raising a rate that is already near the peer norm given the cap.

## 1. What the US funds today

Social Security is the federal budget's largest single lane: in FY2025 it cost
**$1,580,673M**, funded by **$1,283,736M** of dedicated OASDI payroll receipts
[`SRC-OMB-HIST-2-4-FY2027`] — a ~$297B current-year gap covered by trust-fund drawdown.
The tax is **12.4% combined** (6.2% employee + 6.2% employer) on wages up to the
**taxable maximum** ($176,100 in 2025, $184,500 in 2026) [`SRC-SSA-OACT`]. Program cost
is **5.3% of GDP** and projected to rise toward ~6.9% at mid-century peak as the
population ages [`SRC-SSA-TRUSTEES-2026`]. It is a genuinely dedicated, contributory
lane — the clearest case in the budget of a tax tied to a purpose — which is exactly
why its *base* design matters.

## 2. Peer benchmark

US public pension spending is near the middle of the peer distribution, not high
[`SRC-OECD-PENSIONS-2025`]:

| Country | Public pension spend, % GDP | Combined contribution rate |
|---|---:|---|
| Italy | 16.1 | 33% |
| France | 13.4 | ~28% (multi-pillar) |
| Germany | 10.8 | 18.6% |
| Japan | 9.2 | 18.3% |
| Sweden | 8.0 | ~17.2% |
| **OECD average** | **8.1** | — |
| **United States** | **7.3** | **12.4% (capped)** |
| United Kingdom | 7.1 | (no ring-fenced rate) |
| Canada | 5.9 | ~11.9% (capped) |

The US spends slightly *below* the OECD average and taxes at a *below-peer rate*. The
naive read — "raise the rate toward Germany's 18.6%" — misreads the system: peers
generally apply their higher rates to a **far broader or uncapped base**. The US
distinction is not a low rate; it is a **capped base**.

## 3. The cap: the distinctive US feature

Most peer systems tax all or nearly all earnings; the US exempts everything above the
taxable maximum. As a result **~17–18% of US covered earnings are above the cap and
pay no OASDI tax** [`SRC-SSA-TRUSTEES-2026`]. The cap also **erodes** relative to
high-end wage growth: as top earnings have outpaced the median, a rising share of the
wage base has escaped the tax over recent decades. So the US raises less from old-age
contributions than its headline 12.4% suggests — not because the rate is low, but
because a sixth of earnings is outside the base. This is the lever the solvency debate
overlooks.

## 4. The solvency path

The shortfall is real and dated [`SRC-SSA-TRUSTEES-2026`]:

- **OASI trust fund depletion: Q4 2032** (~78% of benefits payable then); the
  theoretical **combined OASDI** fund **~2034** (83% payable at depletion, declining
  toward 65% by 2100). DI is solvent across the 75-year window.
- **75-year actuarial deficit: 4.42% of taxable payroll (~1.5% of GDP).**

This is a structural, demographic shortfall (aging, not a one-year dip). It will be
closed by some combination of more revenue and lower benefits; the question this paper
answers is *which revenue lever* is fair.

## 5. All factors → the recommended lever: the cap, not the rate

- **Solvency.** The 75-year deficit (4.42% of payroll) must be closed; the cap is the
  largest untapped base.
- **Historical/structural.** The cap's erosion is itself a cause of the shortfall —
  restoring base coverage is a natural fix.
- **International.** Peers tax broader/uncapped bases; lifting the cap moves the US
  toward peer base design, not above peer rates.

The three converge on **the cap, not the rate**. Scored options
[`SRC-CBO-BUDGET-OPTIONS`, #60955], 10-year projections net of offsets:

| Option | Scored revenue | Share of the 75-yr shortfall |
|---|---|---:|
| Raise the cap to cover 90% of earnings | ~$0.8T / 10yr | ~20% |
| Apply the 12.4% tax to earnings over $250,000 | ~$1.6T / 10yr | ~70% |

Eliminating the cap entirely closes roughly half-to-three-quarters of the long-range
shortfall (depending on whether the extra earnings also credit benefits). None of
these raises the 12.4% rate. A rate increase, by contrast, would fall on every wage —
including low earners already fully taxed — while leaving the capped high-end base
untouched.

## 6. Distribution

The cap is the most regressive feature of the old-age tax: above the taxable maximum,
the marginal OASDI rate falls to zero, so a worker earning $1,000,000 pays the same
OASDI tax as one earning $184,500. **Lifting the cap is therefore progressive** — it
asks only earners above the cap to contribute on more of their earnings — and it is the
least-regressive way to close the gap. A **rate increase is regressive-to-flat** (it
hits all wages, capital income exempt); a **benefit cut** falls on retirees, hardest on
those most dependent on Social Security. The one design choice within cap reform is
**benefit linkage**: crediting the newly-taxed earnings toward benefits raises future
costs and closes less of the gap; not crediting them (as in the >$250,000 option)
closes more but weakens the contributory link. The paper recommends the cap lever and
flags this linkage tradeoff rather than resolving it.

## 7. The needs, the problem, and the modernization case

**Why the US struggles here.** A dedicated, contributory program is funded on a base
that a rising share of earnings escapes, against an aging population — so the fund
depletes on schedule while the headline rate looks "normal." The problem is **base
erosion**, not an unusually generous program or an unusually low rate.

**The modernization case.** Modernizing the old-age tax means **modernizing its base to
match peer practice**: lift or eliminate the wage cap so the levy applies to high
earnings as peers' do, protect scheduled benefits, and stop treating a rate hike or a
benefit cut as the only options. The contributory promise is kept by broadening *who*
is taxed on *what*, not by taxing low earners more or paying retirees less.

## 8. Reform design: the cap as the lever

In the program-lane model, Social Security is the canonical **dedicated lane** —
payroll in, benefits out, its own trust fund. The lane's operating rule (from the
operating-system work) is **"fix the base, not the rate"** for shortfalls: the
`shortfall_rule` here is satisfied by a cap adjustment with a published distributional
record, not a rate increase or a silent benefit reduction. `allocation_method =
proposed_reform`. The borrowed-share context does not apply (the lane is trust-funded,
though drawdowns ultimately draw on public borrowing — disclosed).

## 9. Discussion and limitations

- **Not a full solvency package.** Cap reform closes 20–70% of the 75-year deficit
  depending on the option; full solvency may also require benefit-formula or
  retirement-age changes, which are out of scope here.
- **Benefit linkage.** Crediting newly-taxed earnings toward benefits reduces the net
  solvency gain; the tradeoff is flagged, not resolved.
- **Behavioral response.** High earners may shift compensation form (to capital or
  deferred income); CBO scores incorporate some response, but realized yields are
  uncertain.
- **Projections.** Trust-fund and deficit figures are Trustees intermediate-assumption
  projections; depletion dates have shifted year to year (2033 → 2032 between the 2025
  and 2026 reports).
- **Unified-budget note.** A trust-fund drawdown is financed by redeeming Treasuries,
  i.e., from public borrowing — so the "trust fund" is not outside the federal cash
  constraint.

## 10. Conclusion

The American old-age tax is not unusually high or unusually generous; it is unusually
**capped**. US public pension spending is near the peer norm and the 12.4% rate sits
below peers — but a sixth of earnings escapes the tax, the trust fund depletes around
2032–34, and the 75-year deficit is 4.42% of payroll. The fair lever is the **cap, not
the rate**: lifting or eliminating it is progressive and scored to close 20–70% of the
shortfall, while protecting benefits and leaving low earners untouched. Modernizing the
old-age tax means modernizing its base to match the peers it is measured against.
