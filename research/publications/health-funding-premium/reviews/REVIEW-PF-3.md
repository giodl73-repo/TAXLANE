# Review — PF-3 Health / Program Economist (Round 1)

**Score: 2.0 — major revisions.**

Summary: disciplined framing; "price not utilization" is correct in the literature;
but the paper asserts more than it defends and the efficiency scenarios are presented
as near-mechanical when they are the hardest maneuver in US health policy.

Strengths: honest scope caveat; well-conceived coverage-floor rule; clean funding-vs-delivery scoping.

- P1: Efficiency scenarios are mechanical (10/20/30% x lane total) with no behavioral/delivery model; abstract/conclusion present them as money "freed without reducing coverage" while §8 concedes the mechanism is out-of-scope and historically fails — internally contradictory. Reframe as conditional ceilings, not deficit-closing dollars.
- P2: "twice the cost for narrower coverage" is evidenced on spending (% GDP) but the coverage/outcome side is asserted — no uninsured rate, no outcome metric (amenable mortality, life expectancy). The cost-per-OUTCOME thesis never quantifies an outcome.
- P3: "price not utilization" needs at least one supporting citation (price-vs-volume decomposition).

TOP_P1: mechanical savings presented as bankable while the mechanism is conceded out-of-scope and failure-prone.
