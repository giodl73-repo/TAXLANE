# A Federal Budget You Can Read — and Balance

**The idea in one sentence:** give every public purpose a named lane with a visible
rate, fund it from a named tax, show the borrowed share honestly, and you can
debate — and balance — the budget in plain sight.

*This is an educational reform model. It uses modeled allocation, not a legal
tracing of your specific dollars, except where dedication already exists in law
(payroll, trust-fund excise). Figures are FY2025, sourced to OMB and IRS.*

---

## 1. The problem: we spend what we don't collect

In FY2025 the federal government spent **$7.01 trillion** and collected **$5.24
trillion**. The difference — **$1.77 trillion, about 25 cents of every dollar
spent — was borrowed.** That borrowing has a cost that is already the third-largest
thing in the budget: net interest, **$970 billion**, larger than the entire
defense budget. Every year we don't balance, that bill grows and crowds out
everything else.

"Spend only what we collect" means closing that 25-cent gap. The rest of this
explainer shows how.

## 2. Two honest facts that frame the fix

- **The US is a low-tax country.** Total taxes are **25.6% of GDP** versus the OECD
  average of **34.1%** — about 31st of 38 wealthy democracies. So the gap is partly
  a revenue choice, not only an overspending one.
- **Health is the one big over-spend.** US government health runs **~14.3% of GDP,
  about twice the OECD's 7.1%**, for narrower coverage. That is a price problem, not
  a coverage problem — and the single largest place to save.

So the honest fix uses **both** levers: collect somewhat more (we have room), and
bend the health price (we overpay). Neither alone is enough.

## 3. Two ways to actually balance it — both keep us a low-tax country

| | Path A (revenue-led) | Path B (balanced) |
|---|---|---|
| Bend health cost | 20% (−$395B) | 30% (−$593B) |
| Defense toward Europe's level | to 2.5% GDP (−$157B) | to 2.0% GDP (−$309B) |
| **Cut / collect mix** | 31% cut / 69% collect | 51% cut / 49% collect |
| **Total taxes, % of GDP** | 25.6% → **29.6%** | 25.6% → **~28.5%** |

**Either path closes the entire $1.77T gap with no borrowing — and still leaves the
US below the OECD average (34.1%), below the UK and Canada.** You can balance the
federal budget and remain a comparatively low-tax country. That is the core finding.

## 4. Where each dollar goes — and who pays

Each lane carries a rate (its share of the dollar) and a named funder. See the
chart `docs/charts/program-lane-rate-model/lane-rate-bar.vl.json` and the full
argument for each rate in `program-lane-rate-cards.md`.

| Lane | Rate | Funded by |
|---|---:|---|
| Social Security | 22.6¢ | its own payroll tax (already dedicated) |
| Medicare | 14.2¢ | payroll + premiums + general |
| Health (non-Medicare) | 14.0¢ | income tax |
| Debt service (interest) | 13.8¢ | income tax (cost of past borrowing) |
| National Defense | 13.1¢ | income tax |
| Income Security & Family | 10.0¢ | income tax + unemployment payroll |
| Veterans | 5.4¢ | income tax |
| Transportation | 2.1¢ | fuel/airport excise (already dedicated) |
| Justice & Government | 1.8¢ | proposed: corporate tax |
| Smaller lanes (8) | ~6¢ total | mostly income/corporate |

The key design choices the model makes about *who* pays:
- **Payroll already funds specific lanes** — Social Security and Medicare. This is
  the proof the idea works; it just isn't shown to you.
- **Corporate tax ($452B) ≈ funds the "commerce & capacity" cluster** — courts,
  infrastructure, science, education, agriculture, diplomacy — the public goods
  business relies on. A volatile tax matched to flexible investment lanes.
- **Income tax stays general and flexible** — it covers the must-fund core where
  the deficit actually sits.

## 5. Rates aren't frozen — here's how they move

A legible system doesn't fix rates forever; it moves them **visibly**. Every change
runs through a trigger, a named decision-maker, and a published explanation showing
the old rate, the new rate, the reason, and the effect on the borrowed-share line
(full model: `docs/research/2026-06-24-rate-adjustment-operating-model.md`).

Three real examples (`rate-change-worked-examples.md`):

- **Defense goes up** because of the 2025 Hague 3.5% commitment — and the **+$146B**
  is named, not quietly borrowed.
- **Health goes down** by **$395B** through a 20% efficiency gain — coverage intact,
  22% of the deficit closed.
- **Social Security lifts its wage cap** to close a $297B shortfall — the rate and
  benefits unchanged; higher earners pay on more of their wages.

Up, down, or base-only, the citizen gets one legible answer to "why did my rate
change?"

## 6. Why this would actually hold (not just look good)

A balance rule is only real with guardrails (`2026-06-23-balance-rule-guardrail-spec.md`):
accrual measurement, net interest funded first, an independent estimator, an
automatic trigger on **actual** receipts, hard limits on "emergency" exemptions,
and an always-visible borrowed-share line. Transparency alone is not control —
control lives in the appropriation and shortfall rules. The model says so plainly,
because an eight-perspective review insisted on it.
