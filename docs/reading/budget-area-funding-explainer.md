# Where the US Funds Too Much, Too Little, and Just Enough

**The idea in one sentence:** "Are taxes too high or too low?" has no single answer —
the US **over-funds** one budget area, **under-funds** another, sets defense by
**strategy**, runs a peer-normal retirement program on a **broken base**, and
**under-collects overall** — so the honest debate is lane by lane.

*This packet distills six panel-reviewed research papers (see `docs/papers/` for the
PDFs and `research/` for the full text). It is an educational explainer, not tax, legal,
or policy advice. Figures are FY2025 unless noted and are sourced in
`docs/sources/source-version-ledger.md`. Where a paper recommends a direction, that is a
**reform proposal** and a **value judgment**, labeled as such — not a finding the
accounts produce. The "reviewers" behind the papers are AI-simulated review lenses, not
real people.*

---

## What this packet is

Five budget areas, each studied in its own paper, plus a synthesis. For each area the
paper asked the same plain question — *should the US spend more, less, or the same, and
how should it be funded?* — and compared the US to other wealthy democracies, to its own
history, and to what the program actually buys. This packet is the short, plain-language
version. Three labels stay visible throughout:

- **Current law / fact:** what the system does or what the data shows now.
- **Comparison:** how the US stacks up, **on a matched basis** (comparisons are only fair
  when the definitions match — a recurring theme below).
- **Reform proposal:** a possible direction, which is a value judgment needing real
  debate, not an accounting result.

## The one-page picture

Of every dollar the federal government spent in FY2025, about five lines are most of the
budget:

| Lane | Share of FY2025 spending | The one-line story |
|---|---:|---|
| Health & Medicare | **28%** | The US pays about **twice** what peers pay — for worse results. |
| Social Security | **23%** | Spending is **normal** for a rich country; the **tax base** is the problem. |
| Net interest (on the debt) | **14%** | Now **larger than the entire defense budget** — the price of borrowing. |
| National Defense | **13%** | **Above** Europe, **below** the US's own history; a strategic choice. |
| Income Security (the safety net) | **10%** | Family support is **about half** the rich-country norm. |

And underneath all of it: the US **collects less tax than almost any peer** (25.6% of the
economy vs. an average of 34.1%) and **borrows about 25 cents of every dollar it spends**.

## 1. Health — we pay double for less

**Fact.** Government and required health spending in the US is about **14% of the
economy** — and the federal Health and Medicare lanes alone are **28% of all federal
spending**, the single biggest thing the government does.

**Comparison.** Other wealthy democracies spend about **half** as much of their economy
on health (about 7%), and they **cover everyone**. The US leaves about **8% of people
(27 million) uninsured** and has a life expectancy about **2.7 years below** the rich-
country average. The extra American spending is not because Americans use more care — it
is because **prices** (for procedures, drugs, and administration) are higher.

**Reform proposal.** The problem is **price, not the tax rate**. The paper argues *not*
for raising health taxes but for an **efficiency-first** approach that brings the price of
care toward peer levels while guaranteeing coverage. Any savings are **conditional** — they
depend on actually lowering verified prices, not on the funding lever alone.
*(Full paper: "The American Health Funding Premium.")*

## 2. Social Security — the program is normal, the base is broken

**Fact.** Social Security is the second-biggest lane (**23% of spending**). The payroll
tax is **12.4%**, and the trust fund is projected to run short around **2032–2034**, after
which scheduled benefits couldn't be fully paid without a change.

**Comparison.** US public pension spending (**~7.3% of the economy**) is **right around
the rich-country norm** (~8.1%), and the 12.4% rate is actually **lower** than in big peer
systems (Germany 18.6%, Japan 18.3%). The catch: the US only taxes wages **up to a cap**
($176,100 in 2025), so a **growing share of high earnings — about a sixth — is never
taxed** for Social Security.

**Reform proposal.** Fix **the base, not the rate**. Lifting or removing the cap is the
most progressive way to close most of the shortfall, without raising the 12.4% rate on
ordinary workers or cutting benefits. *(This rests on a fairness premise — that a more
progressive fix is better — stated openly. Full paper: "The Old-Age Tax and the Cap.")*

## 3. Defense — a strategic choice, not an accounting one

**Fact.** Defense cost **$916 billion** in FY2025 (**13% of spending**), about **3% of the
economy**.

**Comparison.** That is **above the European norm** (~2% of GDP) but **far below US
history** (about 14% during the Korean War, 6–7% under Reagan). NATO's 2025 "Hague"
commitment aims for **3.5%** of GDP on core defense by 2035.

**Reform proposal.** Honestly, the accounts **can't pick the right number** — the level is
a **strategic judgment** about threats and commitments. The paper's answer is a **range
(about 2.0–3.5% of GDP)**, not a single figure, and it declines to recommend a cut on
"we spend more than Europe" grounds alone, because the US underwrites a global role Europe
does not. *(Full paper: "The Defense Tax in Allied Perspective.")*

## 4. The safety net — thin where children are

**Fact.** Income security — unemployment, food and housing aid, refundable tax credits,
SSI — is **10% of spending** ($702 billion).

**Comparison.** Overall US social spending is only **a little below** the rich-country
average (and **above** it once private spending like employer health is counted). But
**direct support for families and children** is the exception: about **1.1% of the economy
even after counting tax-credit help, against a norm near 2.4%** — roughly half. And much
US family help runs through the **tax code** (the Child Tax Credit, the EITC), which makes
it **unstable**: in 2021 an expanded, fully-refundable Child Tax Credit helped push child
poverty to a **record-low 5.2%**; when it lapsed (along with other pandemic aid), child
poverty **more than doubled to 12.4%** in 2022.

**Reform proposal.** **Strengthen and stabilize** family support, and make it **durable**
(a steady benefit rather than stop-go tax credits). *(This rests on the premise that
cutting child poverty is worth funding, stated openly; the expanded-credit episode was a
major but not the only cause of the 2021–22 swing. Full paper: "The Thin Safety Net.")*

## 5. Revenue — a low-tax country with a borrowing habit

**Fact.** In FY2025 the government spent **$7.01 trillion**, collected **$5.24 trillion**,
and **borrowed the $1.77 trillion difference — about 25% of spending**.

**Comparison.** The US collects **25.6% of its economy in taxes (all levels of
government)** versus an OECD average of **34.1%** — roughly **31st of 38** wealthy
democracies. It lacks the broad national sales tax (VAT) and the higher social-insurance
rates that fund peer governments.

**Reform proposal.** Treat the deficit as **two-sided** — a spending issue *and* a revenue
gap — and lean on **broadening what is taxed** (curbing tax breaks, the Social Security
cap, a possible VAT) rather than only raising rates. Even closing the whole gap on the
revenue side would still leave the US **below** the peer average. *(Full paper: "A Low-Tax
Country with a Borrowing Habit.")*

## Putting it together

There is **no single "taxes too high / too low" answer**, because the lanes are different
problems pointing different ways:

- **Health** — spend the same, **buy more** (fix price).
- **Social Security** — hold the rate, **fix the base** (the cap).
- **Defense** — a **strategic band**, not a number the budget picks.
- **Family support** — **spend more**, and make it durable.
- **Overall** — **collect more**, because a quarter of everything is borrowed and the
  interest bill now tops the defense budget.

**One honest warning that runs through all of it:** comparisons are only fair when the
**definitions match**. "The US spends 14% on health" (which counts required private
insurance) cannot be added to "Social Security is 7% of GDP" (a different measure) or
"defense is 3%" (a budget-category measure). Each lane has to be read **on its own basis**
— which is exactly why a single tax number hides more than it shows.

**What TAXLANE proposes** is not a single verdict but a **way of seeing**: give every
public purpose a named lane, and show its **level**, its **fair comparison**, its
**borrowed share**, and **what it buys** — so the public can argue about each lane on the
facts, instead of about one number that answers none of the five real questions.
