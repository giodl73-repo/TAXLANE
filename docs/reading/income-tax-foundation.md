# Income Tax Foundation

## What this packet is

This is the first public TAXLANE reader. It explains the income tax as a civic
and budget system: where the authority came from, how the tax is calculated
today, how receipts enter the federal budget, and what it would mean to show
public-purpose lanes.

This packet is not tax advice, legal advice, accounting advice, or a calculator.
It does not tell any person what they owe. It explains the public system that
turns income-tax law into receipts, spending, borrowing, and public debate.

## The short version

The income tax is not one simple pipe from a taxpayer to one program. It is a
legal tax instrument, a return calculation, a payment system, a federal receipt
source, and then part of a budget process.

The central TAXLANE rule is:

> A taxpayer-facing claim about what income tax paid for must say whether it is
> legally dedicated, proportionally allocated, deficit-inclusive, illustrative,
> or a proposed reform lane.

Without that label, a chart can look clear while saying more than the accounting
supports.

## 1. How the income tax got here

The federal income-tax story has several distinct events:

| Period | What happened | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Civil War | Congress used an early income tax as a wartime revenue tool. | The first income tax was not the original peacetime baseline. |
| 1872 | The Civil War income tax ended. | Federal income tax was not continuous from the 1860s to today. |
| 1894-1895 | Congress tried another income tax, and Pollock created a constitutional constraint. | The legal authority question became central. |
| 1913 | The Sixteenth Amendment and Revenue Act of 1913 created the modern post-amendment income-tax foundation. | Authority and statute should be explained separately. |
| World War II | The tax base broadened and withholding made income tax a mass wage-earner experience. | Many people experience income tax through paychecks before filing. |
| Modern code | The Internal Revenue Code organizes current law. | The code is the current law surface, not the original constitutional source. |

The key lesson is that authority, rates, collection, and spending are different
layers. A history of the income tax should not skip those layers.

## 2. How the tax works for a taxpayer

For a public reader, the current income-tax system is easiest to understand as a
chain:

1. A person receives income.
2. The person reports income and other items on Form 1040 and schedules.
3. Adjustments and deductions help determine taxable income.
4. Marginal brackets apply in layers.
5. Credits reduce tax due.
6. Withholding and estimated payments count as payments toward the tax.
7. Filing reconciles tax, payments, refundable credits, refund, or balance due.

Two common mistakes matter:

| Mistake | Better wording |
|---|---|
| "Your whole income is taxed at your bracket rate." | A bracket rate applies only to the income in that bracket layer. |
| "Withholding is the tax." | Withholding is a payment toward the final tax calculation. |

This packet does not calculate liability. It explains the moving parts that
must be understood before building a taxpayer receipt.

Sources behind this section are recorded in
`docs/research/2026-06-21-current-system-explainer.md`.

## 3. What happens after tax is paid

After payment, income tax becomes a federal receipt. But federal receipts are
not all the same:

- individual income taxes,
- corporation income taxes,
- payroll and social-insurance taxes,
- excise taxes,
- fees and other collections,
- borrowing.

Federal budget accounting also distinguishes receipts from budget authority,
obligations, outlays, trust funds, special funds, and offsetting collections.
Those words are not interchangeable.

The current source-backed finding is:

> Ordinary income tax is mostly a general fund receipt. That means it is
> generally not legally tagged to one program before Congress and budget law
> determine spending.

So "what did my income tax pay for?" needs a method label.

## 4. Four honest allocation labels

TAXLANE uses four labels before showing a taxpayer-facing allocation:

| Label | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Legal dedication | Law credits a receipt source to a fund or purpose. |
| Proportional allocation | A model spreads a taxpayer payment across outlay categories by share. |
| Deficit-inclusive allocation | A model shows both tax-funded and borrowed shares of spending. |
| Proposed reform lane | A future statutory design could link a tax to a public purpose. |

The label is not a footnote. It is part of the claim.

## 5. Why deficits must be visible

If the federal government spends more than it receives in a fiscal year, current
receipts did not cover all current outlays. Borrowing filled the gap.

That means a taxpayer receipt that allocates only taxes across program spending
can hide part of the story. A complete civic receipt needs a deficit or borrowed
share lane, plus debt service as its own visible cost.

The borrowed share is not a tax lane. It is a warning that public commitments
can exceed current receipts.

## 6. What public-purpose lanes are for

TAXLANE's advocacy position is taxpayer legibility:

> People should see the public-purpose lanes their taxes support, with each
> lane labeled by legal status, spending category, fiscal condition, and whether
> the allocation is actual, modeled, deficit-inclusive, or proposed.

First TAXLANE lanes include:

- Constitutional Government and Justice.
- National Defense and Security.
- Veterans and Service Commitments.
- Health and Care.
- Retirement and Social Insurance.
- Income Security and Family Support.
- Education, Work, and Civic Capacity.
- Infrastructure, Transportation, and Places.
- Natural Resources, Energy, and Environment.
- Commerce, Housing, and Market Stability.
- International Affairs and Diplomacy.
- Debt Service and Past Commitments.
- Borrowed Share / Deficit Gap.

These lanes are not all current legal destinations for ordinary income tax.
They are a way to make public purposes visible using budget functions, receipt
sources, and spending-control labels.

## 7. Three model tiers

TAXLANE separates three ideas that are often blended together:

| Tier | What it does |
|---|---|
| Visibility receipt | Shows the current system with labeled allocations. No law changes. |
| Budget presentation lane | Standardizes public reporting around lanes. Tax law may not change. |
| Statutory program-linked tax lane | Creates a legal link between a tax stream and a public purpose. |

The first public product should be the visibility receipt. Statutory lanes are
more serious because they can affect rates, appropriations, benefits,
shortfalls, reserves, borrowing, and filing burden.

## 8. What any statutory lane must answer

Before TAXLANE treats a program-linked tax lane as a proposal, the lane must
answer at least these questions:

1. What is the tax base?
2. What is the rate rule?
3. Which fund or account receives collections?
4. Does spending need annual appropriation?
5. What happens if receipts fall short?
6. What happens if receipts exceed needs?
7. Can reserves be borrowed, swept, or reallocated?
8. Can Congress override the dedication?
9. What happens to beneficiaries during shortfalls?
10. What burden does it add for taxpayers, employers, preparers, IRS, Treasury,
    OMB, and program agencies?

If a lane cannot answer those questions, it is not ready to be a reform
proposal.

## 9. What TAXLANE should build next

The foundation wave supports three next products:

1. A source-backed data dictionary for rates, receipts, and outlays.
2. A public visibility receipt with placeholder amounts and method labels.
3. A lane crosswalk from OMB functions to TAXLANE public-purpose labels.

The order matters. TAXLANE should make the current system legible before asking
whether the system should be redesigned.
