# Historical Funding Evolution Note

## Purpose

This note summarizes what TAXLANE learns from source-reviewed OMB Table 2.4
milestone rows. It is descriptive historical budget accounting, not a taxpayer
receipt and not a reform proposal.

## Reviewed Milestones

| Year | Source-backed lesson |
|---:|---|
| 1940 | Social-insurance receipts already include trust-fund and federal-fund components; excise receipts are federal-fund rows in this first covered year. |
| 1957 | Disability insurance and transportation trust-fund excise receipts are visible as distinct streams. |
| 1966 | Hospital insurance appears as a social-insurance subcomponent. |
| 1983 | Excise trust-fund detail includes transportation, airport and airway, black lung, inland waterway, and hazardous substance superfund rows. |

## What We Are Learning

Historical funding is layered:

- Parent receipt categories hide important subcomponent differences.
- Social-insurance receipts are not ordinary individual income-tax receipts.
- Trust-fund and off-budget labels appear inside receipt categories and need to
  be preserved as source labels.
- Federal-funds rows cannot be treated as automatically general-fund rows.
- Excise taxes evolve from federal-fund-only detail in 1940 into mixed
  federal-fund and trust-fund detail in later milestones.
- Total receipt categories are useful for reconciliation but are too mixed for
  legal allocation claims.

## Product Implication

TAXLANE can show a public historical funding story, but it must be a layered
story:

1. Start with parent receipts by source.
2. Expand categories into subcomponents where OMB provides detail.
3. Preserve fund treatment, off-budget labels, and missing markers.
4. Only then discuss any public-purpose lane, and only with an allocation-method
   label.

This strengthens the public explanation of why ordinary income-tax receipts
should not be casually mapped to Social Security, Medicare, unemployment,
transportation, airport, environmental, or other dedicated streams.
