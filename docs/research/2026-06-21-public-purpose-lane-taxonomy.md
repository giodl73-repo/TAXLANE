# Public-Purpose Lane Taxonomy

## Decision supported

This note defines the first TAXLANE advocacy/design position: TAXLANE
legibility should move from one opaque general-revenue label toward visible
public-purpose lanes. The lanes are not claimed as current legal dedication.
They are a proposed standard for taxpayer-facing explanation and future reform
design.

## Research question

What lanes should taxpayers see if the income tax is explained as funding public
purposes, and how should those lanes relate to traditional federal spending
categories such as discretionary spending, mandatory spending, functions, and
trust funds?

## What the current budget already tracks

The federal budget already has spending lanes, but they are not usually shown to
taxpayers as "your tax paid this public purpose."

OMB Table 3.1 groups federal outlays by superfunction and function. The major
recurring structure includes:

- National Defense.
- Human resources:
  - Education, Training, Employment, and Social Services.
  - Health.
  - Medicare.
  - Income Security.
  - Social Security.
  - Veterans Benefits and Services.
- Physical resources:
  - Energy.
  - Natural Resources and Environment.
  - Commerce and Housing Credit.
  - Transportation.
  - Community and Regional Development.
- Net interest.
- Other functions:
  - International Affairs.
  - General Science, Space, and Technology.
  - Agriculture.
  - Administration of Justice.
  - General Government.
- Undistributed offsetting receipts.

OMB Tables 8.5 and 8.7 then separate much of spending into mandatory and
discretionary programs. This matters because "taxpayer choice" is not the same
in every lane. Some lanes are annual appropriations; some are statutory
commitments; some are debt service; some are dedicated trust-fund-like flows.

## Advocacy position

TAXLANE should advocate this standard:

> People should be able to see the public-purpose lanes their taxes support,
> with each lane labeled by legal status, spending category, fiscal condition,
> and whether the allocation is actual, proportional, deficit-inclusive, or a
> reform proposal.

The goal is not to make every taxpayer choose every line item. The goal is to
make compulsory funding legible enough that citizens can debate the size,
purpose, and accountability of each lane.

## Proposed top-level taxpayer lanes

These are the first proposed lanes for a taxpayer-facing standard. They are
designed to map to OMB functions while using language a public reader can
understand.

| Lane | Public question answered | Traditional OMB fit | Default legal status today |
|---|---|---|---|
| Constitutional Government and Justice | What funds the constitutional machinery, courts, law enforcement, tax administration, and general government? | Administration of Justice; General Government; portions of other functions. | Mostly general fund / appropriated. |
| National Defense and Security | What funds defense, military readiness, security obligations, and defense-related commitments? | National Defense; some International Affairs and Veterans interfaces. | Mostly discretionary/general fund. |
| Veterans and Service Commitments | What does the country owe to veterans and prior service members? | Veterans Benefits and Services. | Mix of mandatory and discretionary; mostly not ordinary TAXLANE dedicated. |
| Health and Care | What funds health programs, public health, Medicare interfaces, Medicaid, and care supports? | Health; Medicare; portions of mandatory health programs. | Mix of trust-fund, mandatory, discretionary, and general fund. |
| Retirement and Social Insurance | What funds retirement, disability, unemployment, and social-insurance commitments? | Social Security; unemployment compensation; federal retirement; social insurance receipts. | Often trust-fund or dedicated payroll/social-insurance tax, not ordinary income tax. |
| Income Security and Family Support | What funds food, housing, family support, refundable credits, and safety-net commitments? | Income Security; parts of Human Resources. | Mostly mandatory/general fund plus program-specific rules. |
| Education, Work, and Civic Capacity | What funds education, training, employment, social services, science, and long-term human capacity? | Education, Training, Employment, and Social Services; General Science, Space, and Technology. | Mostly discretionary/general fund, with some mandatory programs. |
| Infrastructure, Transportation, and Places | What funds roads, transit, airports, water systems, community development, and place-based investment? | Transportation; Community and Regional Development; some physical capital tables. | Mix of trust funds, excise taxes, discretionary/general fund. |
| Natural Resources, Energy, and Environment | What funds land, water, energy systems, agriculture, environmental protection, and resource stewardship? | Energy; Natural Resources and Environment; Agriculture. | Mix of discretionary/general fund, special funds, fees, royalties, and offsetting collections. |
| Commerce, Housing, and Market Stability | What funds housing credit, deposit insurance, market stabilization, commerce, and business-like federal activity? | Commerce and Housing Credit; offsetting collections/receipts. | Mixed; often netted, credit, fee, or offset-heavy. |
| International Affairs and Diplomacy | What funds diplomacy, aid, alliances, global commitments, and international institutions? | International Affairs. | Mostly discretionary/general fund. |
| Debt Service and Past Commitments | What pays interest and costs from prior borrowing? | Net Interest; debt-related budget concepts. | General financing obligation; not a program benefit lane. |
| Borrowed Share / Deficit Gap | What current spending is not covered by current receipts? | Deficit from receipts minus outlays. | Not a tax lane; required context lane. |

## Mandatory vs discretionary view

Taxpayer lanes should also carry a spending-control label:

| Control label | Meaning | Why taxpayers need it |
|---|---|---|
| Discretionary | Generally funded through annual appropriations. | Shows what Congress revisits regularly. |
| Mandatory | Governed by standing law, eligibility, or formulas. | Shows commitments that continue without annual line-by-line appropriations. |
| Net interest | Cost of past borrowing. | Shows the cost of earlier policy choices and deficits. |
| Offsetting / fee-supported | Costs are reduced or financed by collections recorded as offsets. | Prevents fees, premiums, and business-like receipts from being confused with taxes. |
| Trust-fund or dedicated | Collections are credited to a legally designated fund or purpose. | Shows where a lane has statutory dedication, while still requiring trust-fund caveats. |

## What should change

### 1. Taxpayers should get a receipt with lanes, not just a total

The ordinary taxpayer should see:

- total individual income tax liability,
- total payroll/social-insurance tax liability when available,
- proportional outlay allocation across public-purpose lanes,
- borrowed share of the same spending year,
- legally dedicated taxes separated from modeled general-fund allocation,
- notes for mandatory/discretionary/trust-fund status.

### 2. The receipt should not hide deficits

If a year has a deficit, the receipt should show that current spending exceeded
current receipts. Otherwise the receipt makes taxes look like they funded the
whole government when some obligations were shifted to borrowing.

### 3. Existing dedicated taxes should be named plainly

Payroll taxes, Medicare-related taxes, gas taxes, aviation taxes, premiums, fees,
and other dedicated or offsetting flows should not be blended into ordinary
TAXLANE allocation. The standard should show them as different financing
mechanisms.

### 4. Reform lanes should be auditable

If reform proposes a lane such as "Justice Tax" or "Infrastructure Tax," the
lane must include:

- tax base,
- rate rule,
- receipt account,
- appropriation rule,
- outlay target,
- surplus/reserve rule,
- shortfall rule,
- borrowing rule,
- override rule,
- beneficiary impact,
- compliance burden,
- annual public audit.

### 5. Some lanes should stay shared

Not every public purpose should become a narrow dedicated tax. Some functions
need general-revenue flexibility. The standard should distinguish:

- **receipt lanes**: actual legal financing streams;
- **visibility lanes**: public-purpose categories for taxpayer understanding;
- **reform lanes**: proposed statutory dedication or rate/base changes.

## Candidate reform architecture

The first reform architecture should not set rates yet. It should define three
layers:

1. **Baseline receipt**: describes today's system using OMB functions and actual
   receipt sources.
2. **Legibility receipt**: gives the taxpayer a proportional and
   deficit-inclusive lane view without changing law.
3. **Statutory lane model**: explores which portions of income tax, if any,
   should become program-linked taxes with explicit rules.

## Role review implications

| Role | Required challenge before lane adoption |
|---|---|
| Taxpayer Advocate | Is the lane understandable and not advice-shaped? |
| Budget Accountant | Does the lane state actual vs modeled allocation and spending-control status? |
| Source Custodian | Is the lane grounded in OMB/IRS/Treasury/CBO/USAspending source records? |
| Public Goods Steward | Is the public purpose legitimate and clearly distinct from fees or private benefits? |
| Program Beneficiary Reviewer | Does the lane protect continuity and explain shortfall impacts? |
| Compliance Burden Reviewer | Does the lane add forms, calculations, employer burden, or administrative cost? |
| Fiscal Sustainability Reviewer | Does the lane expose deficits, debt service, and long-term commitments? |
| Reform Skeptic | Does the lane disclose fungibility and avoid false precision? |

## Adopt now

- Use the top-level taxpayer lanes as the public-purpose taxonomy for future
  receipts and reading packets.
- Keep OMB functions as the source-backed crosswalk behind the plain-language
  lane names.
- Add spending-control labels to every lane.
- Keep the borrowed share/deficit gap visible.

## Prototype next

- Build a lane crosswalk table from OMB Table 3.1 functions to TAXLANE lanes.
- Build a receipt method that allocates TAXLANE payment across these lanes by
  proportional outlay share.
- Build a second method that adds deficit-inclusive borrowed share.

## Defer

- Rate recommendations.
- Final lane names.
- Any claim that these lanes are already legal TAXLANE destinations.
